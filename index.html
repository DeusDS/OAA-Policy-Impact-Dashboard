SCENARIO A — If OGC Waits Until ARC Has Its Fully Executed DAS Contract

Under this scenario, A&IS does not submit subgrantee contract requests to OGC until after ARC receives its fully executed grant from DAS (historically September). Using average processing times — 31 days for OGC to draft, 51 days for providers to return, and 15 days for final ARC execution — subgrantees cannot begin invoicing until late October through late December, creating significant back-billing obligations.

SCENARIO B — Actual Practice: Early OGC Submission, Hold Pending DAS Execution

Under ARC's current practice, A&IS submits subgrantee contract requests to OGC in April or May — months before DAS executes the master grant. OGC drafts the contracts and providers sign them, but A&IS holds all partially executed contracts until ARC receives the fully executed DAS contract. Only then are subgrantees issued their final countersigned contracts and permitted to begin invoicing. This results in minimal back-billing and protects ARC financially.

📋 Summary for Legal & Executive Review

  • ARC's current early-submission practice (Scenario B) reduces subgrantee back-billing exposure from 3–5 months (Scenario A) to 0–2 months per fiscal year.
  • Over the past four fiscal years, early submission has avoided $7.5M–$8.9M in annual back-billing obligations that subgrantees would otherwise need to bring accounts current.
  • The A&IS "hold" mechanism — retaining partially executed contracts until DAS execution — provides the legal safeguard while preserving the financial benefit of early preparation.
  • Requiring OGC to wait for DAS execution before drafting (Scenario A) would push final subgrantee contract execution to October–December every year, compressing the service delivery and invoicing window and creating 3–5 months of back-billing pressure on all subgrantees simultaneously.
  • The data supports formalizing the early-submission policy to ensure continuity of this risk management practice across future fiscal years.
